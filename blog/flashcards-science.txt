1a:T5fd,<p>In a world of adaptive algorithms and gamified apps, recommending a deck of paper cards can sound quaint. But strip away the novelty, and learning research keeps pointing at two mechanisms that flashcards execute almost perfectly.</p><h2>1. Active recall: the flip is the lesson</h2><p>When a child sees a question and must <em>produce</em> the answer before flipping the card, their brain does something fundamentally different from recognising an answer among four options. Retrieval practice — pulling information out rather than putting it in — is one of the most robust effects in cognitive psychology. The flip is not a reveal; the flip is the workout.</p><h2>2. Spacing: forgetting is part of the plan</h2><p>Cards make spaced repetition physical. The 'got it' pile and the 'again tomorrow' pile are a spaced-repetition algorithm a five-year-old can operate. Each time a nearly-forgotten card is recalled, the memory trace comes back stronger.</p><h2>What about screens?</h2><p>Apps can implement both mechanisms — the good ones do. But for young children the medium carries costs: notification-shaped distraction, passive tapping that mimics recall without producing it, and one more negotiation about screens. A card asks for nothing except attention.</p><h2>The parent effect</h2><p>The quiet advantage of physical cards is you. Sitting across from your child, celebrating the flip, laughing at a silly answer — learning wrapped in attachment is learning that sticks.
