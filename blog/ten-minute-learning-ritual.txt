1a:T6e1,<p>Every September, thousands of well-meaning parents draw up beautiful study schedules. By October, most are abandoned — not because parents don't care, but because the plans were designed for a family that doesn't exist: one with quiet evenings, predictable energy and no surprise meetings.</p><h2>Why ten minutes wins</h2><p>Cognitive science has been consistent on this for decades: <strong>spaced, short practice beats long, rare practice</strong> — dramatically. A child who plays with number cards for ten minutes a day learns more in a month than one who does a ninety-minute worksheet marathon every other weekend.</p><p>Ten minutes has another superpower: it survives real life. It fits between dinner and bath. It fits in the car. It fits the days when everyone is tired, which — let's be honest — is most days.</p><h2>Anchor it to something that already happens</h2><p>The most reliable rituals don't rely on willpower or reminders. They attach to an existing anchor:</p><ul><li><strong>Breakfast cards</strong> — three cards while the toast is toasting</li><li><strong>Car cards</strong> — a set that lives in the seat pocket</li><li><strong>Bedtime flip</strong> — two cards after the story, as a wind-down</li></ul><p>Pick one anchor. Not three. One ritual that happens is worth ten that were planned.</p><h2>Let your child run it</h2><p>The moment your child starts saying 'it's card time!' — and they will — hand over the ceremony. Let them fetch the box, deal the cards, decide who goes first. Ownership is rocket fuel for consistency.</p><p>Start tonight. Three cards, five minutes, no pressure.
